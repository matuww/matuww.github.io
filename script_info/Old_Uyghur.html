Epigraphy1

Square script2

Usually used for religious and literary documents in 9 - 14 c. CE.
It is characteristic of having distinct letterforms due to being carefully written.

Old Turkic manuscript fragment. Undated. Toyuk, East Turkestan.
(Description - here)

Semi-square script3

A less formal variant of square script.

Manuscript from German Central Asia expeditions. Undated.
Tocharian script also written alongside Old Uyghur script.
(Description - here)

Cursive script4

Used in parallel with square script and for rapid writing.
Became common to write civil documents in this style after 12 c. CE.

Manuscript in codex form in Old Turkic in Uyghur script. 1350 CE. Dunhuang Mogao, China.
(Description - here)

Block-print5

Block-printing adopted the square script as the foundation for its form when Old Uyghur was adapted for this purpose in 14 c. CE.
This style is also subsequently used for inscriptions.

Manuscript, ink on paper. 1450 - 1550 CE.
(Description - here)

Post-Mongolic script6

Horizontal orientation influenced by Arabic in Afghanistan and Anatolia after 14 c. CE.
It was used for literary and civil documents.

Kutadgu Bilig. Written in Karakhanid. 15 c. CE copy.
(Description and translation - here)
